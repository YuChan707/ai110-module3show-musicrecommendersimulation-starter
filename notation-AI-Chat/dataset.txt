Why the top song always wins
Every time there is a genre + mood + close energy triple match, the score clusters near 3.97–3.99 out of 4.0. That pattern repeats across every profile because the math guarantees it.

Score breakdown for "Sunrise City" (Pop Happy profile):

Rule	Calculation	Points
Genre: pop == pop	exact match	+2.00

The song wins because it is the only song in the catalog that hits all three rules simultaneously. The algorithm is working correctly — but the structure of the weights creates a ceiling problem explained below.

Is genre weighted too heavily?
Yes — and the data proves it.

The worst-scoring pop song ("Romantic Serenade") still outscores the best non-pop song ("Rooftop Lights"):


Romantic Serenade  (pop, wrong mood, far energy)  →  2.65
Rooftop Lights     (indie pop, right mood, close energy)  →  1.96
A song with the right genre but wrong mood and mediocre energy beats a song with the right mood, close energy, and a nearly identical genre ("indie pop"). This is the bias: genre alone (2.0 pts) is worth more than mood + near-perfect energy combined (1.0 + ~0.96 = 1.96).

The structural problem:


Max score WITH genre match:     2.0 + 1.0 + 1.0 = 4.0
Max score WITHOUT genre match:        1.0 + 1.0 = 2.0
A genre match floors your minimum score at 2.0 even if everything else is wrong. A non-genre song can never exceed 2.0 even if everything else is perfect. The genre gate controls 50% of the total possible score.

Is it bias or limited dataset variety?
Both — but the weights amplify the dataset's thinness.

The catalog has 15 distinct genres across 20 songs, meaning most genres have only 1 song. When your genre matches, that single song wins automatically. There is no competition within the genre to force meaningful differentiation.

Three concrete improvements
1. Reduce genre's dominance — rebalance the weights

Current max: 4.0 pts. Proposed rebalance:

Feature	Current	Suggested	Reason
Genre	2.0	1.5	Still highest, but no longer a floor that dominates
Mood	1.0	1.0	Keep — mood is context-critical
Energy	1.0	1.0	Keep
Tempo	—	0.75	Already in your CSV, currently unused
Valence	—	0.5	Already in your CSV, currently unused
This brings the max to 4.75 and means a no-genre song with perfect mood + energy + tempo + valence can score 3.25 — genuinely competitive with a genre-only match.

2. Use the features already in the CSV

Your dataset has tempo_bpm, valence, and danceability that score_song currently ignores entirely. Adding them at lower weights immediately increases differentiation without changing the dataset.

3. Expand the catalog

With 1–2 songs per genre, a genre match is essentially a pre-determined winner. Adding 5–10 songs per genre forces the mood and energy rules to actually break ties — which is what they are designed to do.
